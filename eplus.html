Japan's Premier Ticketing, Sales & Distribution Application


ePlus is one of Japan's largest and oldest ticketing, sales and distribution companies, servicing millions of music lovers, sports fans, theater enthusiasts and more each year.

With a rich 20 year history, ePlus was one of the first Japanese ticketing sales companies to take advantage of the World Wide Web for ticketing sales and distribution. Their rich history however also belied an incredibly bloated design ecosystem -relics from years of design and development artifacts liter the experience.


As a result of servicing a wide range of venues, artists, performers, athletic events as well as complying with Japanese government regulations concerning electronic sales of tickets, warnings, and disclaimers, ePlus is built on a mammoth case of business logic. As pain points naturally grew, users were being slowed down and hindered from purchasing tickets.


ePlus needed to increase their conversion rate but also needed to maintain their SEO copy and database structure.


For this task, ePlus partnered with Est-Rouge and Senkō Labs for a complete redesign of the UI/UX for the ePlus mobile app, along with building a software development kit to be integrated into Spotify.jp and future investment opportunities.

UI/UX Design and flow

One of the greatest challenges we faced was preserving the rich copy of ePlus while also needing to simplify the experience for the user. Because ePlus has specialized agreements with many of the agencies, venues, and performers that use their platform, we needed to create a system that was both restrained and flexible

Example of the information included in one disclaimer page, in this case, the Attention Note that comes before entering the purchasing flow. All of the copy must be preserved in the mobile application.


Redesign of the Attention Screen

With several rounds of different user testings for the selection and payment process, we were able to see which functions people understood well and the parts that they were hesitant about. We picked up some really valuable information and feedback from the users that we tested. After, we chose parts that worked the best with our users and polished them down into one design for more user testings.


Below is the synthesis of the data we accumulated and the conclusion we reached:

Comparing the architecture from before and after, we changed a lot of steps and structures for the selection to completion process to be better, faster and more efficient. The steps that customers needed to take reduced by half amount thanks to combining previously separate parts of the process and minimizing where and how information appears on screen. The users are well guided throughout the whole process from start to finish without any distractions or feeling overwhelmed by the mountain of information they previously felt.
